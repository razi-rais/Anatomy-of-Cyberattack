Bots Protection: Turnstile

How a modern bot check verifies a real visitor before your server accepts a form, with no puzzles to solve.

The scenario

The attack

Forms are a favorite target for bots. Automated scripts hammer sign up pages, login forms, comment boxes, and checkout flows to create fake accounts, stuff stolen passwords, post spam, scrape data, or drain inventory. A plain form has no way to tell a real person from a script, so it accepts every submission.

What Turnstile does

Turnstile sits in front of the form. It runs quiet checks in the visitor's browser, looking at signals that are hard for a bot to fake, and issues a short lived token only when it trusts the visitor. Your server then asks the vendor to confirm that token before it accepts the action. A human passes without solving anything. A bot is stopped.

The green steps are the real gate. The browser token alone is not trusted. Your server must confirm it with the vendor, so a bot that skips the widget and posts a fake token is still rejected.

How Turnstile decides: bot or human click to expand

All three modes run the same checks. They differ only in whether the visitor sees a widget or is ever asked to click. The checks gather signals that are cheap for one real browser to produce but hard for a bot to fake at scale, then score them to decide whether to issue a token.

Browser integrity
Probes many browser and device APIs (navigator, window, rendering) to confirm a real, consistent browser and to spot automation tools like Selenium or Puppeteer, or a headless browser.
Lightweight proof of work
Tiny background computations that are trivial for one visitor but expensive for a bot trying to pass millions of times.
Reputation and threat intelligence
The vendor weighs what it already knows about the visitor's IP, network, and past challenge history across its global network.
Connection and timing signals
Low level details of the connection and the timing of the checks differ between a real browser and a script.
Optional behavior signals
It may consider gestures like mouse movement, but it never requires you to solve a puzzle.
Adaptive decision
If the signals look clearly human, the visitor passes silently. If something looks risky, Managed mode can ask for a single checkbox before issuing the token.

Architecture at a glance. Three parts, three separate decisions, made in three places. The numbers below match the steps under the diagram.

  1. Page loads (browser). Your page includes the widget. Its script runs in the visitor's browser.
  2. Browser challenge (browser and vendor). The widget runs its checks and exchanges signals with the vendor's challenge service. Decision A, by the widget: is this visitor trustworthy enough to receive a token? A human passes, a bot gets no token.
  3. Token issued (browser). If trusted, the widget puts a short lived token in a hidden field named cf-turnstile-response. The token is at most 2048 characters, valid for 5 minutes, and usable only once.
  4. Form submit (browser to your server). On submit, the browser sends the form data plus the token to your server.
  5. Your server calls Siteverify (server to vendor). Your server POSTs to the Siteverify API with two required values, your secret key and the token. The secret key never leaves your server.
  6. The vendor validates the token (vendor). Decision B, by Siteverify: is this a real, fresh, unused token for my site? It checks the TOKEN, not the visitor. The human or bot judgment already happened in step 2. Siteverify only confirms the token is authentic, was issued against your secret key, has not expired, and has not been used before. It returns success true or false plus the fields below. A reused or expired token returns success false with timeout-or-duplicate.
  7. Your server decides (your server). Decision C, by your server: allow or block. If success is true, accept the action. If false, reject it.

What the Siteverify response contains. Your server reads success to decide, and can use the rest for extra checks.

Why your server must do steps 5 to 7. The vendor states that the client side widget alone does not protect your form. Tokens can be forged, expire after 5 minutes, and are single use. Only the server side check confirms the token is real, which is why a forged token is rejected in the demo above.

What the vendor does on its end, and what it never sees click to expand

Two things happen on the vendor's side, in two phases.

Phase 1, while the widget runs. The widget gathers signals in the browser and exchanges them with the vendor's challenge service. The vendor evaluates those signals (browser integrity, lightweight proof of work, reputation from its global network) and, if it trusts the visitor, mints a short lived, cryptographically signed token tied to your sitekey. Here it sees the challenge signals and which site asked (your sitekey and the page hostname).

Phase 2, when your server calls Siteverify. The vendor receives only the token, your secret key, and optionally the visitor IP. It checks the token signature to confirm it is authentic and was issued for your widget, confirms it has not expired, and confirms it has not been used before. Here it sees just the token and your secret.

The mental model:

  • The vendor is not reading your form data at any point. The fields the user typed, the page payload, your database, none of that is sent to the vendor.
  • During the challenge it looks at the visitor's browser signals, not your page content.
  • During validation it looks only at the token, plus your secret key to authenticate the request and identify the widget.
  • The token is an opaque, signed receipt that says the vendor checked this visitor, at this time, for this site. Your server trusts it only after Siteverify confirms it is genuine.
Protecting a mobile app click to expand

What you do depends on whether it is mobile web or a native app.

  • Mobile web (a website in a phone browser). It works exactly the same, with no extra steps. The widget runs in the mobile browser and your server validates the token as usual.
  • Native app (iOS, Android, React Native, Flutter). Turnstile does not run natively, because it needs a browser to run its JavaScript challenges. The vendor's supported approach is a WebView, a browser component embedded in your app, that loads a page containing the widget. The token is then sent to your server and validated with Siteverify, exactly as before.

WebView requirements, from the vendor:

  • JavaScript enabled and the DOM storage API available.
  • Network access to challenges.cloudflare.com.
  • A consistent User Agent for the whole session. Changing it mid session makes the challenge fail.
  • Any Content Security Policy must still allow challenges.cloudflare.com.

Your server side does not change. Whether the token came from a desktop browser, a mobile browser, or a WebView inside a native app, your backend still calls Siteverify with the token and your secret key, and makes the same allow or block decision.

Turnstile vs a traditional CAPTCHA

QuestionTraditional CAPTCHATurnstile
Who does the workThe user solves a puzzle, like picking traffic lights or reading warped text.The browser is checked automatically. Usually nothing to solve.
User frictionHigh. Puzzles are slow, frustrating, and often fail on the first try.Low to none. Most visitors pass without a single click.
AccessibilityImage and audio puzzles are hard for many users.No puzzle to solve, and it meets WCAG 2.2 AAA.
PrivacySome CAPTCHAs track users across sites for advertising.No tracking for advertising, and no personal data sold.

In one line: a CAPTCHA asks the user to prove they are human by solving a puzzle. Turnstile asks the browser to prove it, so the user usually does nothing. Turnstile is a CAPTCHA alternative, not a different kind of puzzle.

Where reCAPTCHA fits: CAPTCHA vs reCAPTCHA vs Turnstile click to expand

Think of CAPTCHA as the category, and reCAPTCHA and Turnstile as two products inside it.

  • CAPTCHA (early 2000s). The original idea: a test to tell humans from bots, usually distorted text you type. It is the umbrella term, not a single product.
  • reCAPTCHA (Google). The long dominant product. v2 (December 2014) is the "I am not a robot" checkbox with image puzzles when unsure. v3 (October 2018) shows nothing and returns a score from 0.0 to 1.0 that you act on, but leans on Google cookies and cross site signals. Its tradeoffs are friction (the v2 puzzles) and privacy (Google tracking and ad ecosystem). Documentation: developers.google.com/recaptcha
  • Turnstile (Cloudflare, September 2022). The newer challenger with the same goal, aiming to remove both tradeoffs. Usually no puzzle, and privacy preserving. The vendor states it never uses cookies, does not track user behavior, and does not rely on browsing history. It offers a similar API so teams can migrate from reCAPTCHA.

Where reCAPTCHA stands. It is the established, widely used option and still very capable. Its cost is user friction and privacy. Turnstile keeps the protection while dropping the puzzle and the tracking. CAPTCHA is the concept that both belong to.
